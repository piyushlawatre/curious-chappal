# Viral Social Commentary Format

Use this file whenever a topic is a current Indian internet controversy where opinion is split. In this lane, the debate is the entry point. The script's job is to steelman both sides and then surface the structural shift that produces the disagreement — not to declare a winner.

The lane calibration sits in `REFERENCE_SCRIPTS.md`: Reference 15 (The 70-Hour Work Week) and Reference 16 (No Kids / One Kid). Read both before drafting.

---

## When This Lane Applies

Route to Viral Social Commentary when all three are true:

1. A specific viral object exists: a public statement, a service, a price, a job listing, an app feature, a platform rule, a generational lifestyle pattern, or a public behaviour.
2. The Indian internet is already arguing about whether it is right, wrong, healthy, exploitative, or simply a sign of something larger.
3. The Short cannot land honestly without naming both sides *and* the structural shift that produced the argument.

Example topics that fit:
- Generational labour debates (Reference 15: the 70-hour-week proposal).
- Demographic and lifestyle shifts (Reference 16: urban Indians choosing no children or one child).
- Platform-economy flashpoints (platform fees, tipping debates, gig-worker conditions, dating-app pricing tiers).
- Sudden norm changes that one half of the audience celebrates and the other half resents.

Example topics that do *not* fit:
- A topic with a clean hidden mechanism that the audience is not arguing about → route to Real Reason, Smart Money, Science Lite, or Sharp Contradiction.
- A topic where one side is genuinely indefensible (frauds, scams, criminal cases) → route elsewhere; this lane requires two steelmannable sides.
- A topic that is purely party-political (electoral, partisan, religious-community) → out of scope. See "Failure Modes" below.

---

## Mandatory Six Beats

A Viral Social Commentary script is incomplete unless it contains all six:

1. **Frame the debate.** One or two lines that name the viral object and what the disagreement is about. The hook does not need a specific number — Reference 15 opens with the framing ("The Indian seventy-hour week debate is a fight between two generations who agree on almost everything except the math.") rather than a price tag.
2. **What the viral object actually is.** Two to three concrete lines. Direct quotes from app copy, job listings, public posts, or the original statement go here when the language itself is the proof. *Note: Beat 2 may be fused with Beat 1 when the framing itself describes the viral object — as in Reference 16, where the structural pattern (couples without children) is described within the opening framing. A distinct Beat 2 is most important in Mode A (math-anchored debate) when the receipt is the argument. In Mode B (structural-shift debate), Beat 1 and Beat 2 may naturally merge.*
3. **Side A, steelmanned.** The strongest version of one side's argument, with at least one concrete reason or specific. The script speaks Side A's case the way Side A would speak it — not a strawman.
4. **Side B, steelmanned.** The strongest version of the other side's argument, with at least one concrete reason or specific. Same standard.
5. **Both sides are right — inside their own world.** A single beat that grants both sides their internal coherence. Reference 15: "Both sides are right — inside their own economy." Reference 16: "Both arguments have a real audience."
6. **The mechanism beneath.** The structural shift that produces both arguments — the generational economy that no longer overlaps (Ref 15), the lifestyle that became visible for the first time (Ref 16). This is the lane's signature move: the disagreement has a cause, and the cause is bigger than the debate.

The closing line is its own block (see "Closing Line" below).

---

## Closing Line

The close is three beats, in this order:

1. **A one-line compressed insight.** A single sentence that names what the fight is actually about. Reference 15: "The fight is not really about hours. It is about which India still exists in 2026." Reference 16: "A child used to be the default. It is now a line item that competes with every other line item."
2. **A single viewer question.** Specific. Stakes-naming. Reference 15: "Which India do you live in?" Reference 16: "Which side made the argument you actually nodded at?"
3. **A one-line nudge to share with a named audience.** Specific. Names the kind of person who should see this. Reference 15: "Send this to the friend who picked a side before you finished the sentence." Reference 16: "Send this to the friend who already mailed you the spreadsheet."

This three-line shape is lane-exclusive. Do not use it outside Viral Social Commentary. Do not collapse the three lines into one or two. Do not end on an aphorism.

## Two Calibration Modes

The references show that this lane has two calibration modes, both valid:

**Mode A — Math-anchored debate.** When the viral object has a hard number that is the proof (a wage, a price, a fee, a billing line), keep the math visible in Side B. This is the Carryment-era shape. Use it when the receipt is the argument.

**Mode B — Structural-shift debate.** When the disagreement is generational, economic, or lifestyle-based and there is no single number that anchors it, the mechanism beneath the disagreement is structural rather than mathematical. References 15 and 16 are this mode.

In either mode, all six beats above are mandatory. The difference is what populates Side B and the mechanism — a number-based argument or a structural-shift argument.

---

## Direct Viral Evidence Rule

When the viral payload is a job listing, app copy, a rate card, a public statement, a quoted post, or product terms, use the original language directly where it carries the proof.

The reference scripts use this rule even when the source is a public statement, not a price tag. Reference 15 attributes the opening claim ("A senior Indian founder said the country needs young people to work seventy hours a week") plainly. The Short is not the place to hide the receipt.

---

## What This Lane Is *Not*

- **It is not "current event as doorway."** The doorway pattern uses a current event to reach a timeless mechanism. Viral Social Commentary uses a current debate to reach a structural shift. The mechanism is not timeless — it is dated, contemporary, and named for what year and which India produced it.
- **It is not party-political commentary.** Socio-economic debate is in scope. Wage math, generational economics, platform-labour conditions, demographic shifts, lifestyle visibility — all in scope. Government, party, electoral, religious-community side-taking — out of scope.
- **It is not neutral journalism.** The script names the structural shift the debate sits on. It does not pretend the debate has no answer; it just refuses to nominate the audience's answer for them.

---

## Tone

The host names the argument clearly, steelmans both sides honestly, and surfaces the structural cause. Sharp, restrained, slightly uncomfortable. Not preachy. Not a news anchor. Not a polished explainer.

The discipline is to grant both sides their internal coherence and then point at the larger thing producing both — not to sand off the conflict.

---

## Failure Modes

- **Stopping at "both sides are real."** This is the lane's most common failure. Steelmanning is necessary but not sufficient — Beat 6 (the structural shift) is what separates a Viral Social Commentary Short from a centrist hedge.
- **Picking a party-political side.** Government, party, electoral, religious-community — out of scope for this lane.
- **Substituting one side's framing.** Giving Side A's reading and skipping Side B (or vice versa) breaks the lane.
- **Collapsing both sides into a neutral hedge.** "Worker pay is a question that matters" is not steelmanning. Name the specific argument, with a specific reason.
- **Ending on an aphorism.** "Market ka jugaad ab rate card pe aa gaya" or any writerly kicker breaks the closing shape.
- **Forced Hindi / Hinglish anywhere.** This lane follows the same language rule as every other lane — complete Indian English. See `LANGUAGE_AND_VOICE.md`.
- **Treating the viral object as a doorway to a timeless observation.** That is the Real Reason lane. This lane's payoff is *dated* — it names the current structural shift, not a universal principle.

---

## Validation Checklist

Before returning any Viral Social Commentary script, confirm:

- The viral object and the disagreement are framed in the first one or two lines.
- The script names the live debate (the internet is split, the comments are arguing, two generations disagree).
- Side A is steelmanned with at least one concrete reason or specific.
- Side B is steelmanned with at least one concrete reason or specific.
- The "both sides are right — inside their own world" beat is present.
- The mechanism beneath the disagreement is surfaced — the structural shift that produces both arguments.
- The close is three beats: one-line compressed insight, single viewer question, one-line nudge to share with a named audience.
- No timeless-hidden-reason drift. The payoff is dated.
- No aphorism close. No "what do you think?" close. No generic share prompt.
- No party-political side-taking.
- No Hinglish anywhere. Complete Indian English throughout.
