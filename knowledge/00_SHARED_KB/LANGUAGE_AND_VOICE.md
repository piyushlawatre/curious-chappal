# Language and Voice

This file controls how every script sounds. The benchmark is `REFERENCE_SCRIPTS.md`. Every rule here is observable in the sixteen reference scripts.

---

## Language: Complete Indian English

The default language is **complete Indian English**. That means:

- Globally understandable English, shaped by Indian context, Indian examples, and Indian stakes.
- Conversational but not casual. Intelligent but accessible.
- Clean enough to voice over without edits. Sharp enough to hold retention.
- No Hindi or Hinglish. No internet-speak. No "bro / guys / bhai". No emojis. No exclamation marks. Hinglish does not appear in any of the sixteen reference scripts. If a line feels weak and the fix that comes to mind is to add a Hindi word, the line is weak. Fix the English; do not add the Hindi.
- Contractions are rare. The references default to "do not", "is not", "did not". A contraction is used only when the spoken rhythm demands it.

The reference scripts contain zero Hindi words across all sixteen scripts. Indian words appear only as terms of art the assumed viewer already uses without translation: lakh, crore, raga, dal, Mangalyaan. They are not Hinglish. They are English-language usage by Indian speakers.

If the writer feels tempted to add a Hindi anchor to make a line "land", that is a sign the English line is weak. Fix the English line.

---

## Why Not Hinglish

Hinglish was the earlier brief. It is no longer. The decision is grounded in what the reference scripts do, which is what the channel publishes.

- The sixteen reference scripts contain no Hinglish lines, no Hindi anchors, no Hindi closers.
- Hinglish creates a category problem. The channel sits between loud Hinglish fact-creators and dry English documentary-explainers. The differentiation is *register*, not vocabulary mixing.
- An Indian audience watching English Reels and Shorts does not need Hindi cues to feel addressed. Specificity ("Rockefeller University's biology lab", "Mangalyaan", "450 crore rupees") does that work better than vocabulary code-switching.

If a script feels like it needs Hindi to land, the topic, the angle, or the mechanism is doing too little work. Strengthen those.

## Register: The Three Traps and Their Fixes

The voice sits between three traps. Each trap has a script-grounded counterpart from the references that shows the right move.

- **Too academic.** *Wrong:* "This phenomenon constitutes a paradigmatic instance of incumbent path-dependence." *Reference (Nokia, Ref 4):* "This is what is called incumbent capture: when existing revenue becomes so large that it prevents the organization from building what comes next."
- **Too casual.** *Wrong:* "Nokia basically died because they didn't get it." *Reference (Nokia, Ref 4):* "Nokia did not lose to the iPhone. Nokia lost to Nokia."
- **Too Westernised.** *Wrong:* "In Silicon Valley, we call this the innovator's dilemma." *Reference behaviour:* the scripts never reach for a foreign idiom to name an idea. They describe the mechanism in plain words and name it themselves.

## Sentence Rhythm

Every rule here is taken from the reference scripts.

- **Short declaratives.** Target 8–12 words per sentence; hard cap 15 unless a factual claim genuinely requires more (see § Below-Average English Comprehension Rule). Long sentences are reserved for context, never for the punch.
- **Single-sentence paragraphs as punctuation.** *"Freedom. In French."* / *"Your parents did."* / *"So India waits."* / *"That is what we picked."* Use sparingly. They land because the surrounding paragraphs are normal length.
- **Parallel triplets for force.** *"Not one name. Not one location. Not one contact."* / *"Diet does not shift it. Soap does not mask it. Perfume does not cancel it."* / *"No negotiation. No appeal. No second chance."* One triplet per script is plenty.
- **Numbers stated specifically and with units.** "ten months", "six weeks", "thirty years old", "450 crore rupees", "over a hundred million diabetics". Vague quantifiers only when no specific number is needed.
- **Authority named, never vaguely invoked.** "Rockefeller University's biology lab", "WHO treatment guidelines", "the advertising agency N. W. Ayer in 1938". Never "scientists say" or "studies show".
- **Three numbers in a row blur into noise.** The references rarely stack more than two numbers in a paragraph. Convert the third to a non-number: "a fraction of that", "well past every other state", "around seventy to ninety percent". Precision is in the important numbers; the surrounding quantities should breathe.
- **No adjective stacking, no adverb hype.** The drama is in the facts, not in "incredibly", "shocking", or "mind-blowing".

## Repetition Discipline

A thesis stated once with force is stronger than the same thesis restated three or four times in fresh words. Across the reference scripts, the core idea lands once in the body and once more at the close — not in between.

**The rule: say it once strong, restate it once at the close, cut everything between.** Reinforcement is good; the third and fourth restatement is padding that flattens the back third of the Short and, worse, makes every Short feel like the last one.

- Counting test: if your one idea ("X is not about A, it is about B") appears more than twice in different words, delete the surplus.
- This also reduces self-cloning. The surplus restatements are usually the same two-part-inversion shape, so cutting them removes the padding and the sameness in one move.
- The close may restate the thesis. The middle may not — the middle moves the story forward with new facts only.

## Closer Variety

The two-part inversion close — "It is not about A. It is about B." / "X did not lose to Y. X lost to Z." — is the channel's signature, and a signature loses power when every Short uses it. Keep a running ledger of the closer shape used on each published Short in `SLATE_LEDGER.md` (the persisted slate memory; read it at Stage 1 and Stage 3, append every new Short at lock). If the last two Shorts in that ledger closed on the two-part inversion, the next one must close differently (a single-line reframe, a twist-to-personal, a quiet stand, a forward question — see § Closing Line). Vary the shape on purpose.

**Batch rule (mandatory for multi-script runs).** When several scripts are generated in one run, the batch is also the ledger: no closer shape may be used more than twice in any five consecutive scripts, and the two-part inversion may never close two consecutive scripts in the batch. Check this across the batch before returning, not per script.

## Vocabulary Control

A complex term is allowed only when the script explains it immediately with a concrete example. Prefer the same spoken line. The immediately following short line may carry the explanation when no other idea intervenes.

> If a term needs a textbook to explain, the script must do that explanation in the next sentence.

How the references handle this:

- *Ozempic (Ref 8):* "Ozempic is a hormone your body already produces. Every time you eat, your gut releases a hormone called GLP-1." Term named, immediately defined.
- *Nokia (Ref 4):* "This is what is called incumbent capture: when existing revenue becomes so large that it prevents the organization from building what comes next." Term named, defined in the same line.
- *Mosquitoes (Ref 2):* "The mechanism is carboxylic acids, a compound your skin produces naturally as part of your microbiome." Term, what it is, where it lives.

Patterns observable in all sixteen scripts:

- One technical term per script as the default. Additional terms are allowed only when essential and each gets the same immediate explanation treatment.
- Where an abstract noun ("incentive", "structure", "system") appears, a specific example sits in the same paragraph.
- Short verbs over Latinate ones. The scripts use "use", "show", "run", "build", "lose", "wait", "ship", "buy". Never "utilise", "demonstrate", "operationalise".

The premium feeling does not come from heavy words. It comes from a sharp idea expressed cleanly. The references prove it.

## Reader Benchmark (Vocabulary Ceiling)

The assumed viewer is a metro Indian aged 18–40 who reads English daily but did not study in a fully English-medium school. Calibrate vocabulary to a **Class 10–12 educated reader** — not an engineer, not an MBA, not someone who reads The Economist.

### The Core Rule

**If a word is not part of everyday spoken Indian English, replace it or define it immediately in the same sentence. No exceptions.**

This is not about technical or academic terms only. It applies to every word in every sentence — including words that feel "normal" in written English but are not words people actually say out loud.

Ask before using any word: *Would I say this word out loud in a casual conversation with a college friend from a small town?* If the answer is no, or even maybe, replace it.

### The Test, Not a List

One test decides every word: *would I say this word out loud, in a casual conversation, to a college friend from a small town?* If no — or even maybe — replace it, or define it immediately in the same sentence. There is no master list to memorise; the test is the rule.

Watch three habits rather than categories of vocabulary:

- **Academic and domain terms** ("inert", "carcinogen", "oxidise") — obvious; replace or define inline.
- **Newspaper register** — words that feel normal in print but are never spoken ("dearth", "exacerbated", "precipitous", "remits", "disburses"). If it sounds like a headline and not like speech, swap it.
- **History-and-events register** ("besieged", "annexed", "agrarian", "hoarded") — describe the condition in plain words instead ("cut off from food", "took control of by force", "grain traders kept back to sell later at higher prices").

The ten swaps that catch the most real failures:

| Say this instead | Not this |
|-----------------|----------|
| thing that happens | phenomenon |
| spreads quickly | proliferate |
| shortage | dearth |
| made worse | exacerbated |
| used | utilised |
| showed | demonstrated |
| got rid of | abolished, eliminated |
| cut off from outside | under siege / besieged |
| goes stale / reacts with air | oxidises / rancid |
| people who worked on other people's land | agrarian labourers |

**Calibration guard (mandatory).** This rule must PASS all ten anchor scripts in `REFERENCE_SCRIPTS_CORE.md` unchanged. A word the references speak is not banned: "incumbent capture" (defined inline, Ref 4), "executed" (Ref 1), "authoritarian" (Ref 6), "cannibalize" (Ref 4) all pass because each is either everyday for this audience in context or defined in the same line. The test is spoken-register fit plus immediate inline definition — never a lookup table applied mechanically. A flagged word that is defined in its own line passes.

### What To Do When in Doubt

**When in doubt, use the simpler word.** There is no penalty for being too clear. There is always a cost to being too complex.

If two words mean the same thing, use the shorter and more spoken one:
- "Died of hunger" is always better than "succumbed to starvation."
- "Cut off" is always better than "besieged."
- "Pushes out" is always better than "bulges."
- "Showed" is always better than "demonstrated."

You do not need a complete list of every difficult word. The principle is enough: **if it sounds like a newspaper headline and not like spoken conversation, replace it.**

### Definitions Must Be Immediate and Spoken

If a term must stay — because it is the subject itself, like "IVF" or "GLP-1" — define it in the same line when possible. The next short spoken line is allowed only when it directly completes the definition and no other idea intervenes.

- Wrong: *"Nitrogen is inert."*
- Right: *"Nitrogen does not react with food — which is why it keeps chips from going stale."*

The viewer who does not know the word should understand the full sentence without pausing. If they have to pause, the line has failed.

## Below-Average English Comprehension Rule

This is the umbrella doctrine for the Reader Benchmark above and the Instant Comprehension Gate below.

Every script must be understandable to a curious Indian Tier 1 / Tier 2 viewer aged 18–40 who may have below-average English comfort. The viewer is curious and modern, but they should not need academic English, business jargon, trade vocabulary, or prior domain knowledge to understand the video.

The script must be understood on first listen.

The goal is not to make the script childish. The goal is to make it **clear, concrete, premium, and easy to follow at voiceover speed**.

### Simple English Without Losing Premium Tone

Use simple English, but keep the tone confident and intelligent.

A script should sound like:

- a sharp storyteller
- explaining a surprising mechanism
- in clean spoken English
- without sounding academic, childish, motivational, or over-explained

Do not use difficult vocabulary to create a premium tone.

Premium tone should come from:

- clear structure
- strong contrast
- precise facts
- concrete images
- short confident sentences
- earned payoff

Premium tone should not come from:

- trade jargon
- academic English
- long sentences
- abstract business language
- clever but unclear lines

### Sentence Length

Target sentence length: 8–12 words.

Hard cap: 15 words, unless a factual claim genuinely requires a longer sentence.

A listener can lose the thread after 12–15 words at voiceover speed.

Short sentences improve clarity and make the voiceover sound more confident.

### Vocabulary Rule

Remove domain-specific words unless absolutely necessary.

Replace abstract or trade-heavy words with concrete common-English phrasing. These extend the Reader Benchmark swap table:

| Avoid | Prefer |
|---|---|
| rough diamonds | uncut stones / uncut diamonds |
| premium stones | big stones / high-value stones |
| established houses | old diamond traders / big traders |
| couriers | people they trusted |
| credit | loans given on trust |
| community credit | loans given on trust, with no paperwork |
| mastering tiny stones | factories built to cut tiny stones |
| global markets | buyers around the world |
| uneconomic | not worth the money |
| arbitrage | buy cheap in one place, sell for more in another |
| low-value segment | the part nobody wanted |
| operating system | the way the business worked |
| supply chain | the journey from mine to buyer |
| export ecosystem | businesses that sent goods abroad |

### Hard Name Cushion Rule

If the script includes a place, person, community, company, law, or technical term that may be unfamiliar, introduce it with a simple cushion.

Examples:

- `In Belgium, there is a city called Antwerp.`
- `Palanpur is a town in Gujarat.`
- `Surat is the city where the cutting happened.`

Do not overload the viewer with multiple unfamiliar names in one line.

### Concrete Image Rule

Whenever possible, explain abstract ideas through visible objects, people, places, or actions.

Prefer concrete images:

| Abstract | Concrete |
|---|---|
| trust-based trade | a bag of diamonds trusted to one person |
| low-value inventory | stones sitting in a corner |
| ignored market segment | what nobody wanted |
| informal finance | loans given on trust, with no paperwork |
| production specialization | factories built to cut tiny stones by the millions |
| consumer relevance | the next diamond you see at a wedding |

Every script should contain at least 2–3 concrete images the editor can show visually.

**Calibration guard:** as with the Reader Benchmark, this rule must PASS all ten anchor scripts in `REFERENCE_SCRIPTS_CORE.md` unchanged. The test is first-listen comprehension for a below-average-English viewer — never a word count or lookup table applied mechanically.

## The Instant Comprehension Gate (Phrase-Level — run on every line)

The Plain Word Test above checks **words**. It is not enough on its own. A line can be built entirely from easy words and still be hard to follow on first listen, because the **phrasing or register** is wrong. The Reader Benchmark has a blind spot exactly here, and lines like *"a 2020 study in the journal Perception found…"* and *"the Smithsonian records the outcome plainly"* slip through it — every word is simple, but the construction is borrowed from a source document, not from speech.

Run this gate on **every line**, testing the whole line as the viewer hears it:

> **"Will an average Indian viewer understand this immediately — on first listen, while the video is playing — without pausing, rewinding, Googling, or needing outside context?"**

If the answer is no, the line is rewritten. If it cannot be rewritten without losing the point, the script is dropped, not shipped. This gate is not about word difficulty; it is about whether the **spoken line lands instantly**. Common failures that pass the Plain Word Test but fail this gate:

- **A common word used as a proper noun.** "the journal *Perception*" — the listener cannot hear the capital P and tries to parse "perception" as a normal word. Stall.
- **An unexplained institution.** "the Smithsonian" — if the viewer cannot place it, it is not authority, it is a second thing to decode mid-scroll. Name what it is, or cut it.
- **Archive / report register.** "records the outcome plainly", "demonstrates a correlation", "as documented in" — nobody says these out loud.
- **Citation furniture that does no work.** Journal names, study IDs, author affiliations the viewer does not need to believe the claim. Move them to on-screen text.
- **Mental-math friction.** Two adjacent ratios with different denominators, or multiple numerical relationships in the first five seconds, can be unclear even when every word is easy.

## Source-Language vs Viewer-Language (Translate, Don't Transplant)

**Do not use report-style, academic, or institutional phrasing just because the source uses it. Convert it into simple, spoken, average-Indian-friendly English while keeping the fact exact.**

The frozen Verified Source Notes handed forward from Stage 1 protect **facts**, not **wording**. Downstream stages must re-phrase source language into spoken language — that is not "tampering with sourced material," it is required. A journal name, an institution, or a citation phrase stays in the *spoken* line only if it does real evidential work for the viewer. Otherwise it moves to on-screen text and the spoken line carries the plain version.

| Source-language (transplanted — wrong) | Viewer-language (translated — right) |
|---|---|
| "a 2020 study in the journal *Perception*" | "a 2020 study on how people react to this" — or "a 2020 study found:" with the journal name shown on screen, not spoken |
| "The Smithsonian records the outcome plainly." | "The Smithsonian — America's biggest museum group — documents it simply." / "History records it just as simply." |
| "positively impacts evaluation and intention to return" | "people like the place more and come back sooner" |
| "research demonstrates a significant correlation" | "researchers found the two move together" |

The test for keeping a name: **does the viewer believe the claim *more* because of this specific name, and can they place it in one second?** If yes, keep it and explain it in the same line. If no, translate it out of the spoken track.

### Authority Placement: Checkable Does Not Always Mean Spoken

Every evidence claim must be checkable. That does not mean every unfamiliar author name belongs in the spoken script.

1. **Speak it:** recognizable, contested, story-central, or credibility-bearing authority ("WHO treatment guidelines", "PVR INOX's annual filing").
2. **Speak the study shape:** when the full names add no viewer value ("a five-experiment 2020 study found...").
3. **Show the full citation:** author, journal, year, and link belong on-screen and in fact-check notes.

Never use "studies show" as a vague substitute for evidence. Never force two unfamiliar names into speech merely to satisfy an authority checkbox.

## Host Archetype

The host is not a comedian, not a documentary narrator, not a lecturer, not an influencer, not an expert claiming credentials, not an over-fascinated podcast guest.

The host is **a sharp Indian reader who has done the work and is sharing the punchline cleanly.** Calm, specific, restrained. Confident because the facts are correct, not because the delivery is loud. The voice the references demonstrate: declarative, authoritative-but-accessible, intellectually generous, never theatrical.

Authority comes from specificity ("Rockefeller University's biology lab"), not credentials. Warmth comes from clarity, not from "yaar" or "bhai" or "guys".

## Energy

Energy is calibrated to topic, not to category. The references span:

- Quiet gravitas (Noor Inayat Khan, Voyager).
- Restrained curiosity (Mosquitoes, Ozempic, Cochrane).
- Sharp business analysis (Nokia, South Korea, ABCD, De Beers).
- Dry observation (IV Drips, Diet Coke).
- Compressed contemporary debate (70-Hour Work Week, No Kids / One Kid).

What is constant across all sixteen: the energy never rises above clean restraint. There is no fake-hype, no breathless retention chasing, no "you guys won't believe this." There is also no documentary slowness or essay-style ponderousness.

The texture is closer to a smart Indian reader telling you the punchline of a long article they just finished — interested, clear, unhurried but not slow — than to any creator archetype on either end.

**Energy scale (1–10).** Stage templates that ask for an Energy number use this mapping:

| Number | Register | Reference examples |
|---|---|---|
| 1–2 | Quiet gravitas | Noor Inayat Khan (Ref 1), Voyager (Ref 10) |
| 3–4 | Restrained curiosity | Mosquitoes (Ref 2), Ozempic (Ref 8) |
| 5–6 | Dry observation | IV Drips (Ref 3), Diet Coke (Ref 7) |
| 7–8 | Sharp business analysis | Nokia (Ref 4), South Korea (Ref 6), De Beers (Ref 13) |
| 9–10 | Compressed contemporary debate | 70-Hour Week (Ref 15), No Kids / One Kid (Ref 16) |

The ceiling is absolute: even a 9–10 stays inside clean restraint — the number sets pace and bite, never volume or hype.

## Hook Rule

First 1 to 3 sentences. Lands a counterintuitive claim or sharp contrast. Specific. No greeting. No "today we'll talk about". No "did you know".

The reference openers, by shape:

- **Counterintuitive declarative.** "Mosquitoes do not bite randomly. They hunt specific people." / "Ozempic is a hormone your body already produces."
- **Relatable setup + flat correction.** "If you have been sick in India, a doctor probably put you on a drip. For most of those situations, the drip did almost nothing."
- **Two-date contrast.** "In 2007, Nokia controlled half the global smartphone market. By 2013, it sold its phone division to Microsoft for almost nothing."
- **Concrete comparison.** "India sent an orbiter to Mars for 450 crore rupees. The Hollywood film Gravity… had a production budget of 644 crore rupees."
- **Counterintuitive identity.** "Britain's most decorated female spy in the Second World War was an Indian princess."
- **Date + tease.** "In 1961, South Korea's military government told its largest companies something no development economist had ever recommended."
- **Three-beat reveal.** "Global supply chains have a priority list. Most of the time, it is invisible. When supply gets tight, it becomes very visible."
- **Scene-first conflict.** Open on a person, place, or action whose conflict contains the curiosity gap. The scene itself is the specific proof; numbers can arrive later.

These shapes are calibration material. New hooks should land in the same family without copying the specific phrasing.

**The second sentence proves, sharpens, or opens the causal promise.** Proof can be a name, date, institution, number, or concrete action inside a scene. But some strong hooks use line two to sharpen the contradiction and bring concrete proof immediately after. That is valid. What fails is abstract restatement: line two cannot merely repeat line one in softer words.

**Specificity is a tool, not the objective.** A verified ratio is not automatically a stronger hook than a sourced human conflict. Prefer the opening that creates the strongest immediate story question while remaining accurate.

**First-five-seconds number rule.** Use at most one numerical relationship in the opening five seconds. If two useful numbers use different denominators or reference groups, move one later or place it on-screen.

**Two-ratio denominator reset.** When two ratios are genuinely needed later in the script, reset the reference frame before the second number arrives.

Good:
"For every ₹100 PVR INOX earned from tickets, it earned almost ₹60 more from food.
And when its food counter sold ₹100 worth, the food itself cost about ₹25."

Bad:
"For every ₹100 in tickets, they earn ₹60 in food. And ₹75 of those rupees is profit."

The reset is necessary, not sufficient. If both numbers slow the spoken line, move one on-screen.

## Pivot and Mechanism

These are the two beats that distinguish an Anchor Short from a Wikipedia summary. Neither is covered by the Hook or Closing Line sections — they are handled here.

**The pivot (Function 3) is a clear turn, not a mandatory sentence shape.** It may be one flat declarative sentence, or it may happen through a sourced decision, contrast, or scene that naturally changes the viewer's understanding. It should be unambiguous and quick. Do not force a separate "That is not what happened" line when the story spine already turns clearly.

**Context is compressed, never dumped.** Two to four short paragraphs maximum before the pivot arrives. If the setup exceeds four paragraphs before the pivot, the script is not compressed — cut, do not compress. A script that dumps context before the pivot reads like a brochure, not an Anchor Short.

**The mechanism (Beat 4) arrives as concrete images, not abstractions.** Every mechanism in the reference scripts is anchored in a physical, observable image the viewer can hold for one second: "A chair occupied for two to three hours." / "Wire compartments to hold each plate, a wheel that rotated them inside a copper boiler, a motor that pumped soapy water at them." / "A warehouse in London." / "A foreign customer who would not pick up the phone." When mechanism sections drift into abstract nouns — "the incentive structure created a feedback loop where..." — they lose the viewer. Name the object, the room, the person, the specific decision. Trade abstract nouns for pictures.

**The mechanism explains WHY, not just WHAT.** A script that explains what happened but not why it happened is a Wikipedia summary. The mechanism section surfaces the hidden incentive, biology, economics, or power structure — the part the viewer did not know.

**The mental model (Beat 6) is earned, not applied.** A mental model is earned when it follows inevitably from the mechanism already shown. Test: if the same model line would fit four unrelated topics without changing a word, it has not been earned — go back to the mechanism. A bad mental model is decorative, generic, or pasted in early. "This is what is called incumbent capture: when existing revenue becomes so large that it prevents the organization from building what comes next." (Nokia, Ref 4) — that line earns itself because the entire mechanism just demonstrated it. Never invent a model the mechanism has not earned.

**The Indian-relevance bridge (Beat 5) must be earned.** Take the Indian beat out of the draft. If the script reads worse without it, keep it. If it reads the same or better, the beat was not earned. Four of the sixteen reference scripts carry no Indian framing at all. Pasted-on Indian relevance is worse than no Indian relevance.

## Closing Line

One or two sentences. Compresses the insight. The references close in a small set of observable ways:

- **Title-thesis restatement.** "Nokia did not lose to the iPhone. Nokia lost to Nokia." / "India's drip is just easier to see."
- **Twist back to the personal.** "Your parents did." (Mosquitoes)
- **Quiet moral stand.** "Noor Inayat Khan did not fit. She deserved better than to disappear."
- **One-line forward question.** "What else in your biology might be doing the right thing, just not for long enough?" (Ozempic)
- **Single-line reframe.** "That is what we picked." (Voyager)
- **Viral Social Commentary close (lane-exclusive).** A one-line compressed insight, a single viewer question, a one-line nudge to share with a named audience. "The fight is not really about hours. It is about which India still exists in 2026. / Which India do you live in? / Send this to the friend who picked a side before you finished the sentence." This shape is only valid in Viral Social Commentary. Do not graft it onto any other lane.

A closing line is never a motivational quote, never a LinkedIn aphorism, never a generic question, never a "like and subscribe" hook.

**After the closing line, stop.** The references never add a sentence after the payoff. Do not re-explain the mechanism. Do not moralize. Do not wrap up. The closing line IS the final line.

## Banned Creator Tics

These signal generic short-form energy and break the register. Do not use in scripts, captions, thumbnails, or editor-facing text:

- "Right?" / "Honestly?" / "But honestly?" (reaction-tease tics — banned in scripts; only "And / But / So / Because" are load-bearing spoken connectives)
- "Let me explain."
- "You know…"
- "So, see…"
- "Yeah, even I was shocked when I found this out."
- "Check this out."
- "I can prove it in 30 seconds."
- "Imagine you're…"
- "While researching for our video on…"
- "We've been studying…"
- "I have studied this for years."
- "I have been following this story for…"
- "As someone who has researched this…"

The reference scripts carry no host-credential framing. Authority comes from specificity — named institutions, specific numbers, verifiable dates — not from the host's claimed expertise.

## CTAs

### Always banned (every lane)

- "Like and subscribe."
- "Comment your thoughts."
- "Let me know in the comments."
- "Should we do a full breakdown?"
- "Drop your views."
- Generic "what do you think?" without a specific stake.
- "Tag your friend."
- "Share this with everybody."

### Permitted only in Viral Social Commentary lane

- A **specific stakes-naming viewer question** that names a stakeholder or a decision the viewer would actually make. References 15 and 16 use: *"Which India do you live in?"* / *"Which side made the argument you actually nodded at?"*
- A **named-audience share prompt** that names the kind of person who should see it. References 15 and 16 use: *"Send this to the friend who picked a side before you finished the sentence."* / *"Send this to the friend who already mailed you the spreadsheet."*

These shapes are lane-exclusive. Outside Viral Social Commentary, they remain banned.

### Spoken Script vs Description / End-Card

The spoken script and the publishing layer are separate. The CTA rule above governs only what is **spoken**.

- **Inside the spoken script:** no CTA by default. End on the payoff; nothing follows the final line (Punchline Protection). The only spoken CTA permitted is the Viral Social Commentary three-beat close above.
- **In the video description, pinned comment, or an end-card:** a standard channel-growth nudge (subscribe, follow) is allowed. It lives in the publishing layer, is never read aloud, and never appears in the voiceover.

Why: a clean stop on a sharp line is what drives the rewatch and the share — a spoken "share this" weakens the ending. The growth mechanic belongs where it does not cost the payoff.

## Anti-Cloning Patterns

The full anti-cloning list lives in `REFERENCE_SCRIPTS.md`. Below are the highest-risk clone patterns — shapes that recur in the references and will make a new script feel like a renamed copy.

**The rule:** Strip the proper nouns from the draft and compare against the sixteen references. If it still sounds like one of them with different nouns, rebuild from the topic's own contradiction.

**Highest-risk clone shapes to avoid:**

1. **The title-echo closer.** "X did not lose to A. X lost to X." This is Nokia's closing line. Do not use this two-clause self-inversion for any other company or story. Write the closer from the specific topic's mechanism.
2. **The "Your parents did." twist-to-personal.** This is Mosquitoes' closer. It works because the mechanism is biological and inherited. Do not paste it onto non-biological topics by engineering a personalisation hook.
3. **Near-verbatim pivot.** "Everyone explains this as X. That is not what happened." Lift the shape — break with the obvious reading in one flat sentence — but write the line from the specific topic's pivot, not from a Ref 4 template.
4. **The "Not X. Not Y. Not Z." triplet in the same position as a reference.** Parallel triplets are a rhythm device, not a signature. If your triplet lands in exactly the same structural beat as a reference triplet, rewrite its position or form.
5. **A mental model lifted from a reference and reattached to different facts.** "Incumbent capture", "forced competition under survival pressure produces excellence", "the signal is what the price tracks" — these are earned by their specific mechanisms. Do not reuse them as ready-made closers for a different topic.
6. **The two-clause inversion for inventor stories.** "History does not erase the invention. It erases the inventor." This is the Cochrane closer shape. A different inventor story needs a closer earned by its own specific erasure, not the same inversion with different names.

## Test Before Recording

Three quick checks against the reference standard:

1. **Out-loud test.** Read the script aloud, in one take, at speaking pace. If you stumble on a sentence, rewrite the sentence, not your delivery.
2. **Strip test.** Remove every proper noun from the draft. If it still reads like one of the sixteen references with different fillers, rewrite the structure.
3. **Hindi-temptation test.** If a line feels weak and the fix that comes to mind is to add a Hindi anchor, the line is weak. Fix the English; do not add the Hindi.

If all three pass, the language is on benchmark.

## Delivery Cue Vocabulary

Every finalized spoken script carries inline **delivery cues** — short bracketed tokens that tell the host how to perform each line. They are written into the script at Stage 2, preserved through Stage 3, and kept verbatim in the Editor Brief §1 locked script. The app reads these exact tokens to render the **Anchor** view (cues shown as colour-coded chips) and the **Script** view (cues stripped for clean reading), so the vocabulary is fixed and closed.

**The only valid cues (case-insensitive):**

| Cue | Meaning | Typical placement |
|---|---|---|
| `[direct]` | Look straight into camera | Opening line; any direct address to the viewer |
| `[no smile]` | Serious delivery | Grave or somber topics (death, famine, tragedy) |
| `[stress]` | Emphasise the next word or phrase | Inline, immediately before the word |
| `[slow]` | Slow down | Before a line that needs to land |
| `[drop voice]` | Lower, firmer closer tone | The final payoff / closing line |
| `[beat]` | Small pause | On its own line, between lines |
| `[pause]` | Longer pause | On its own line, between beats |

**Rules:**

- These seven are the **only** bracketed tokens permitted in a spoken script. Never invent new cue words (no `[whisper]`, `[laugh]`, `[smile]`, etc.).
- Pause and framing cues (`[beat]`, `[pause]`, `[direct]`, `[no smile]`, `[slow]`, `[drop voice]`) sit on their own line. `[stress]` is the exception — it goes inline, directly before the word it emphasises.
- Stay **restrained**: roughly one cue per one-to-three spoken lines. Cues serve delivery, not decoration. A wall of cues reads as performance, which the channel voice rejects.
- Open scripts on `[direct]`. Mark the final closer with `[drop voice]` (or `[drop voice]` after `[no smile]` for grave topics).
- The spoken words must read perfectly with every cue removed — the clean Script view strips them automatically, so cues never substitute for clear writing.
