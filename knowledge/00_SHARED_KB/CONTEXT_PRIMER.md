# Minimum Context for New AI

**Creative benchmark:** `REFERENCE_SCRIPTS.md`. This primer is subordinate. Operational, safety, sourcing, and stage decisions are controlled by their owning canonical files.

Paste this into a new AI chat. It is a compressed summary. Canonical rules live in the deeper KB files.

---

## Channel Identity

A short-form video channel that takes interesting stories — surprising facts, hidden mechanisms, smart observations, useful insights — and packages them sharply enough that the viewer feels a little smarter within ninety to a hundred and twenty seconds.

The channel sits between loud Hinglish fact-channels and dry long-form documentary explainers. The differentiation is *register*, not vocabulary mixing: complete Indian English, restrained voice, mechanism-first storytelling.

This is NOT:
- A long-form documentary-explainer in Short form
- A polished motion-graphic mini-documentary
- A magazine article squeezed into ninety to a hundred and twenty seconds
- A podcast clip pretending to be a Short
- A generic "Did you know" Hinglish fact channel
- A news / lecture / lifestyle / comedy / opinion channel

---

## Audience

Educated urban Indian viewers aged eighteen to forty, comfortable in English, living in Tier 1 or Tier 2 cities (Pune, Indore, Jaipur, Coimbatore, Lucknow, plus the metros), watching during a commute or break.

They will absorb "Rockefeller University's biology lab", "carboxylic acids", "GLP-1", "incumbent capture", and "remittances" on first listen **only when each unfamiliar term is explained immediately.** This is not a license for complex vocabulary. The vocabulary ceiling is a Class 10–12 educated reader; undefined high-register words are a defect, not sophistication. They recognise lakh, crore, Mangalyaan, dengue, Tipu Sultan, and the Gulf job market without explanation.

What they will not tolerate: greetings, "guys / bro / bhai", emojis, filler, fake-hype words, motivational closes, or scripts that pad to seem important.

---

## Language: Complete Indian English

The channel writes in complete Indian English — the register is not Hinglish. No internet-speak. No "bro / guys / bhai". No emojis. No exclamation marks. Contractions are rare. Hinglish does not appear in any of the sixteen reference scripts. Specificity does the work that vocabulary mixing once attempted.

Specificity does the work that vocabulary mixing used to do. "450 crore rupees", "Rockefeller University's biology lab", "WHO treatment guidelines", "Mangalyaan", "the advertising agency N. W. Ayer in 1938" — these are the textures that make a script feel native to its audience.

See `LANGUAGE_AND_VOICE.md` for the full register.

## Production Target

Every produced Short is an **Anchor Short**. There is no second tier.

- Length: 230 to 280 spoken words.
- Duration: 90 to 120 seconds spoken.
- Hard envelope: 230–280 words and 90–120 seconds. Reframe or move formats rather than padding or over-compressing.

---

## Structural Beats

Every Short performs seven narrative functions. Their order may flex when the topic's strongest story spine requires it:

1. **Hook** — one to three sentences. Counterintuitive claim or sharp contrast. No greeting, no "imagine", no "did you know".
2. **Compressed context** — named specifics. Two to four short paragraphs.
3. **Conventional-explanation pivot** — one declarative sentence that breaks with the obvious reading. ("That is not what happened." / "Neither explanation is correct." / "The answer is not medical. It is psychological.")
4. **Real mechanism** — the hidden system, incentive, biology, or power structure. Two to three paragraphs.
5. **Indian-relevance bridge** — only when the topic supports it.
6. **Mental model** — one or two sentences, surfacing the transferable insight the mechanism has earned.
7. **Closing line** — one or two short sentences. Compresses the insight.

The exception is the Viral Social Commentary lane, which uses its own six-beat structure (see below and `VIRAL_SOCIAL_COMMENTARY.md`).

## Format Lanes

Before scripting, declare a lane. Seven canonical, three proposed.

### Canonical (defined in `FORMAT_LANES.md`)

1. **Real Reason** — viewer knows the surface story, not the actual cause.
2. **Hidden India** — Indian-ness *is* the surprise.
3. **Smart Money/Business** — company, market, or money mechanism hides the real story.
4. **Science Lite** — body, science, health, or tech with a simple invisible mechanism.
5. **Sharp Contradiction** — two facts that resolve via a hidden mechanism.
6. **Viral Social Commentary** — a current Indian debate with a structural shift beneath it.
7. **One-off** — Anchor-quality topic that fits no other lane. Requires written justification.
8. **Forgotten Inventor** — named person, credit-erasure story.
9. **Quiet Monopoly** — small set of unknown firms holding disproportionate control.
10. **Status Game** — behaviour or product that exists to signal status.

*(Lanes 8–10 are proposed — calibrated in `REFERENCE_SCRIPTS.md` but not yet formally adopted. Treat the reference scripts as their definition. Do not treat them as equivalent to the seven canonical lanes.)*

## Viral Social Commentary (Lane Exception)

This lane uses a different structure:

1. Frame the debate (viral object + what the disagreement is about).
2. What the viral object actually is.
3. Side A, steelmanned, with one concrete reason.
4. Side B, steelmanned, with one concrete reason.
5. Both sides are right — inside their own world.
6. The mechanism beneath — the structural shift that produces both arguments.

Closing line is three beats: a one-line compressed insight, a single viewer question, a one-line nudge to share with a named audience. Lane-exclusive.

Calibrated by References 15 (The 70-Hour Work Week) and 16 (No Kids / One Kid). See `VIRAL_SOCIAL_COMMENTARY.md`.

## Voice in One Page

- **Hook.** Counterintuitive declarative, two-date contrast, concrete comparison, three-beat reveal, or a rare sharp question when the question itself is the curiosity gap. Generic/rhetorical question openers remain banned.
- **Story force.** A source-backed scene, person, decision, conflict, or object outranks a receipt-first opening when it creates stronger curiosity. When no sourced scene is needed, a safe viewer-world comparison may carry the opening. A sourced proposition is not automatically a pictureable asset.
- **Concrete carrier.** Every script needs a scene, object, comparison, or action chain the editor can show without writing the thesis on screen. Human-choice mechanisms show what the person sees, does, skips, or chooses.
- **Sentences.** Short declaratives. Rarely past 25 words. Single-sentence paragraphs for punctuation, used sparingly.
- **Authority.** Named specifically. "Rockefeller University's biology lab", not "scientists". "WHO treatment guidelines", not "experts believe".
- **Authority placement.** Speak recognizable/story-central authorities; keep unfamiliar author/journal details on-screen while making the claim checkable in notes.
- **Numbers.** Specific with units. "ten months", "450 crore rupees", "around seventy to ninety percent". Never fake precision.
- **Mental math.** At most one numerical relationship in the first five seconds; never switch denominator/reference group between adjacent spoken lines.
- **Vocabulary.** Default to one necessary technical term; additional essential terms require immediate plain explanations. Prefer the same line; the next short line may define the term when no idea intervenes. Beyond technical terms, every word must be accessible to a **Class 10–12 educated Indian reader**.
- **Closing.** One or two sentences. Title-thesis restatement, twist back to the personal, quiet moral stand, single-line reframe — or, in Viral Social Commentary only, the three-beat viewer-question close.

## Factual Discipline

- Every load-bearing claim is sourced. Routine Shorts: 1–2 trusted sources. Sensitive Shorts: 3+.
- Authority is named, never invoked vaguely.
- Quotes are short, attributed, verifiable.
- Soft hedges ("appears to", "estimates suggest", "reportedly") are used when the source warrants them — never as a substitute for sourcing.
- External creators are never sources. See `SOURCE_AND_FACT_RULES.md`.

---

## Banned

- **Words:** SHOCKING, INSANE, MIND-BLOWING, CRAZY (as hype), UNBELIEVABLE, YOU WON'T BELIEVE, EXPOSED, THE TRUTH ABOUT, SECRET, FORBIDDEN, DESTROYED, BOMBSHELL, WORST / BEST EVER, ULTIMATE.
- **Openings:** greetings, "did you know", channel introductions, "today we will talk about", "have you ever wondered", "what if I told you", "imagine if".
- **CTAs:** "like and subscribe", "comment your thoughts", generic engagement bait, "tag your friend", generic "share this with everybody".
- **Endings:** motivational closes, LinkedIn aphorisms, vague rhetorical questions, forced mental-model lines.
- **Language:** Hindi or Hinglish, internet-speak, "bro / guys / bhai", emojis, exclamation marks — banned. The register is always complete Indian English. Hinglish does not appear in any of the sixteen reference scripts.

## Output Format

Every Short delivered as:

1. Production target (Anchor Short).
2. Format lane.
3. Final title.
4. Thumbnail text (3–5 words, all caps).
5. Hook.
6. Payoff one-liner (or, in Viral Social Commentary, the three-beat close).
7. Mental model / framework (or N/A in Viral Social Commentary).
8. Full spoken script (230–280 words, complete Indian English).
9. Host tone notes.
10. Editor notes.
11. Fact-check notes.

## The Six Questions Before Producing

1. Does the opening land a counterintuitive claim, sharp contrast, or pictureable comparison, with no filler?
2. Is the pivot present and the mechanism earned?
3. Is the language complete Indian English — no internet-speak, no "bro / guys / bhai", no emojis, no Hindi or Hinglish?
4. With proper nouns removed, does the draft still feel distinct from all sixteen reference scripts?
5. Were all time-relative claims freshly checked at final lock?
6. If this was a major rewrite, did it preserve or improve the strongest source-backed narrative asset and concrete story carrier?

If any check fails, fix before recording.

## Calibration

Calibrate every script against `REFERENCE_SCRIPTS.md` — the sixteen scripts are the operating definition of an Anchor Short on this channel.

Do not imitate long-form documentary explainers, magazine-article-as-video, podcast clips presented as Shorts, generic Hinglish fact channels, news explainers, or any existing creator's house style. External creators are market leads at most, never style targets.
