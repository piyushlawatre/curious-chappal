# Topic Selection and Angle Guide

Canonical source for topic approval and the 8-Point Shorts Validation. Mental model is an optional bonus, not a gate. Downstream summaries (such as `../00_SHARED_KB/CONTEXT_PRIMER.md`) point here.

## Before You Start

Identify the load-bearing factual claim and grade its fact-risk per `../00_SHARED_KB/SOURCE_AND_FACT_RULES.md § Source Discipline`. Routine factual Shorts need 1–2 trusted sources; sensitive topics need 3+; viral debates need 1 direct viral source + 1 supporting fact source.

Every Short topic must pass the **8-Point Shorts Validation**:

1. **Strong hook stack within first 1–5 seconds** — one sharp line or 2–3 short spoken lines that attack/reverse an assumption, or stage a pictureable comparison whose contrast creates the causal question before context.
2. **Simple curiosity gap** — the viewer instantly wants to know "wait, why?" or "what happened next?"
3. **Clear payoff** — the Short actually delivers an answer / reveal / takeaway. No cliffhanger-as-content.
4. **A relatable or surprising angle** — either the viewer already cares about this, or it flips something they thought they knew.
5. **Easy-to-understand explanation** — clear enough to follow at scroll speed, no jargon, no unnecessary prerequisites. This includes vocabulary difficulty: any word that would require a dictionary lookup for a **Class 10–12 educated Indian metro viewer** counts as a prerequisite. High-register everyday words (“proliferate”, “oxidise”, “rancid”, “dearth”) fail this gate exactly as technical jargon does, unless defined immediately in the same sentence — and per the calibration guard in `../00_SHARED_KB/LANGUAGE_AND_VOICE.md § Reader Benchmark`, a word the reference scripts themselves speak (e.g. “incumbent capture”, defined inline in Ref 4) is not a failing word. See `../00_SHARED_KB/LANGUAGE_AND_VOICE.md § Reader Benchmark` for the plain-word swap table and test.
6. **Strong retention flow** — every 3–6 seconds delivers a new beat; no flat middle.
7. **The viewer feels smarter after watching** — they leave with a fact, an insight, a reason, or a sharp observation worth repeating.
8. **Our own identity in language, tone, framing, or delivery** — complete Indian English, sharp, casually confident; not a long-form documentary-explainer style ported to Shorts, and not a generic Hinglish fact channel.

Passing all eight points clears the creative gate. Final approval still depends on the Stage 1 scoring thresholds, fact-risk, clone-risk, and auto-downgrade rules.

**Mental models are never a scoring gate and must never be forced.** Some topics clearly yield a transferable framework (Nokia → incumbent capture; ISRO → constraint vs abundance); some strong facts, hidden reasons, and viral debates will not. Capture the model when it appears; never invent one. Absence alone cannot change a score or verdict. It may prompt a closer look at the already-scored mechanism, novelty, and payoff gates.

## Acceptable Short Formats

A Short can be built around any of these — all are legitimate channel content:

- **A surprising fact** ("Bamboo is officially labelled a 'grass' in India — and that one law change unlocks a 53 billion dollar market.")
- **A useful insight** ("Most protein brands fake their numbers — here's how blind testing actually works.")
- **A hidden reason** ("Why your phone has zero network at a 20,000-crore airport.")
- **A simple explainer** ("Why Japan puts a slot machine in every department store.")
- **A relatable curiosity** ("Why mosquitoes love some people and ignore others.")
- **A business angle** ("How Lapinos quietly built 600+ outlets while Domino's slept.")
- **A science angle** ("Why phone cameras and pulse oximeters work worse on dark skin — it's physics, not racism.")
- **A history angle** ("The Indian princess who became Britain's most decorated WWII spy.")
- **A current-events angle** ("India's population is dropping faster than people realise — and Kerala is the warning.")
- **A "you know this, but not the real reason behind it" Short** ("You know Trump owns Mar-a-Lago. You don't know how he turned it into a 40-million-dollar cash machine.")
- **A smart observation packaged simply** ("Indian doctors put you on a drip even when you don't need one — and it's not bad medicine, it's psychology.")
- **A sharp contradiction** ("Australia legalised psilocybin. India banned it. The studies say the same thing.")
- **A fast answer to an interesting question** ("Why is bamboo called India's 'real gold'?")
- **A live viral debate** where Indian internet is "in splits" right now ("Delhi startup will rent you a person for ₹149/hour — and the wage math is the debate"). Route to Viral Social Commentary lane.

All of these are valid channel content when they pass the 8-point validation. A natural mental model/framework is a bonus, not a requirement.

## Recurring Format Lane

After a topic passes the 8-point validation, assign one format lane from `../00_SHARED_KB/FORMAT_LANES.md`:

| Format lane | Best for |
|-------------|----------|
| Real Reason | Surface story vs actual cause |
| Hidden India | Indian lived experience, systems, history, infrastructure, business, or behavior |
| Smart Money/Business | Companies, founders, markets, pricing, products, distribution, money mechanisms |
| Science Lite | Body, health, nature, tech, or science curiosities explained one level deeper |
| Sharp Contradiction | Two facts that seem incompatible until the hidden logic explains them |
| Viral Social Commentary | A current viral controversy splitting Indian internet opinion. The debate is the entry point. Both sides are steelmanned; the structural shift beneath the disagreement is Beat 6 and is the payoff. Closes on a stakes-naming viewer question + named-audience share, not a host aphorism. See `../00_SHARED_KB/VIRAL_SOCIAL_COMMENTARY.md` for the full six-beat spec. |
| One-off | Strong topic that passes the filter but does not fit a recurring lane |
| Forgotten Inventor | Named person, credit-erasure story — proposed lane, calibrated in `../00_SHARED_KB/FORMAT_LANES.md` |
| Quiet Monopoly | Small set of unknown firms with disproportionate control — proposed lane, calibrated in `../00_SHARED_KB/FORMAT_LANES.md` |
| Status Game | Behaviour or product that exists to signal status — proposed lane, calibrated in `../00_SHARED_KB/FORMAT_LANES.md` |

If no lane fits, that is a warning signal. Either the topic needs reframing, or it should be explicitly treated as a one-off.

The lane must create distance: new hook, new framing, stronger Indian-metro language, different close, or distinct visual treatment. Translation alone is not enough.

## Mental Model (Optional Bonus)

Some Shorts naturally produce a clean transferable principle in 8 words or fewer (Nokia → incumbent capture; ISRO → constraint vs abundance). Capture it when it appears. Do not force one.

**A transferable model is not required**, but its absence is a diagnostic, not a green light. A sharp fact, hidden reason, or live debate is publishable on its own (see `../00_SHARED_KB/CHANNEL_CONSTITUTION.md § The Lens`).

If a mental model fits naturally, capture it for the brief; the audit checks only that it is natural, never that it exists (`../03_AUDIT_AND_FINALIZE/03_AUDIT_RUBRIC.md § MODEL-INTEGRITY`). Never reject a topic because it lacks one.

Use mental models when:
- The topic naturally produces a clean, named principle in 8 words or fewer
- The principle transfers to multiple other situations
- The principle can be delivered without making the Short feel heavy

Do NOT force a mental model when:
- The topic is fundamentally just a great fact, observation, or explainer
- Trying to extract a framework would slow the Short down
- The "model" would feel manufactured or aphoristic

## Production Target: Anchor Only

Do not classify topics into multiple production targets. The channel now produces one target:

**Anchor Short:** the only production target. Sharp facts, hidden reasons, useful insights, relatable curiosities, smart observations, simple explainers, business/science/current-affairs angles, and live debates all move forward as Anchor Shorts when they pass the 8-point validation.

Anchor is the production target, not a separate creative format. Format lane still controls the structure. A mental model/framework is welcome only when natural; do not force one just because the target is Anchor Short.

## Difference: Interesting Fact vs Channel-Worthy Topic

**Interesting fact, but not channel-worthy:** A fact with no curiosity gap and no clean payoff. The viewer reads it and thinks "okay" and keeps scrolling.

**Channel-worthy:** A fact, reason, insight, observation, or explainer that passes the 8-point validation — hook, curiosity, payoff, identity, retention. It earns the scroll-stop.

The bar is "strong Short", not "deep idea".

## Difference: News Coverage vs Current Event as Doorway

**News coverage (banned):** "Here is what happened today." Time-sensitive, surface-level, expires in a week.

**Current event as doorway (allowed):** The event is the entry point. The Short reveals the system, the hidden reason, or the relatable observation underneath. The Short still makes sense 6–12 months later.

Rule: if removing the current event from the Short would destroy it, it is news. If removing the event still leaves a strong fact / reason / insight standing, it is channel-appropriate.

## What Makes a Topic Too Generic

A topic is too generic if:

- Three other channels (Hinglish or English) have already made it with the same angle
- There is no real curiosity gap — the hook is just a known fact
- The hook reads like clickbait without substance behind it
- The audience already knows both the surface and the supposed twist
- Removing geography wouldn't change anything (the topic is borrowed Western content with nothing added)

Note: a topic being covered before is *not* automatic rejection — if our angle, framing, hook, or identity is sharper than what exists, we can still make it.

**Own-reference clone check (mandatory).** If the topic — or its likely hook, mechanism, or closer — matches one of the scripts in `../00_SHARED_KB/REFERENCE_SCRIPTS.md`, do not approve it as a fresh topic unless the brief states how it differs in at least three of {hook, framing, mechanism angle, closer, visual treatment}. Re-making "Nokia missed the iPhone" or "urban India is having fewer children" repeats a reference and clones the channel against itself. Reject or reframe.

**Saturation flag.** If the topic is one of the internet's most-covered explainers (McDonald's-is-really-a-real-estate-company, Netflix binge-vs-weekly, "Nokia missed the iPhone"), mark it SATURATED in the brief. A saturated topic is approved only with a genuinely lesser-known lead hook (a fresh 2025 number, an Indian-specific angle) that the familiar coverage does not use. Otherwise the ceiling is capped by "I have seen this".

## Indian / South Asian Relevance

Indian relevance belongs naturally when:

1. The topic is genuinely Indian (bamboo law, airport network, IV drips, ISRO).
2. There is a real Indian application of a global mechanism (Korea's policy vs India's 1991 reforms; Ozempic vs India's diabetes burden).
3. Indian audiences have lived experience the Short can leverage (mosquitoes/dengue, fake toothpaste, protein brands).
4. Indian context meaningfully sharpens the angle.

Do NOT:

- Tack on "and in India..." paragraphs to unrelated topics
- Compare to India when the comparison is weak
- Force *party-political* comparisons that could be read as critical of India (socio-economic debate in the Viral Social Commentary lane is in-scope and required where the topic warrants)
- Add Indian framing as performance

If the Indian angle doesn't naturally serve the Short, leave it out. The audience is Indian. The channel is built for them. Not every Short needs to wave the flag.

## Business Stories

Business topics need a clear mechanism to qualify. A transferable principle is welcome only when it emerges naturally from the business mechanism. Strong hook + clear payoff are still required. Examples:

Before approval, identify the narrative proposition, strongest source-backed asset, and likely story spine. For modern business topics, specifically search for a historical adoption moment, resistance scene, founding conflict, market-structure shift, or decision that can make the mechanism visible.

Also identify the **concrete story carrier**: the scene, object, comparison, or action chain that lets the viewer see the mechanism. This is not the same as the sourced proposition.

- A sourced finding such as "health claims can increase willingness to pay" is a proposition, not a pictureable asset.
- A carrier may be source-backed, or it may be a clearly illustrative viewer-world scene such as two similar packets on one shelf.
- An illustrative carrier must not invent a specific event, quote, motive, company action, or measured result.
- If the mechanism depends on comparison, selection, or relative price, the carrier must show the options or reference point. One isolated packet does not stage a choice between two packets.
- **Pictureability Test:** could an editor storyboard the carrier without putting the thesis itself on screen as text? If no, the brief is still too abstract.

✅ **Acceptable:** "How Lapinos took on Domino's", "Mar-a-Lago became a 40-million-dollar club", "Bamboo unlocked a 53-billion-dollar market".

✅ **Naturally model-bearing:** Nokia's incumbent capture, Toyota / Samsung / Hyundai-style stories.

❌ **Still rejected:** Generic founder-origin biography with no curiosity gap, surface "company did X" stories with no hidden reason.

## Current Affairs Rules

Current-affairs Shorts split into three categories — pick which one applies before scripting:

### (a) News coverage — banned
Surface "here is what happened today" reporting. Expires in a week. Never produce.

### (b) Current event as doorway (Real Reason / Hidden India / Sharp Contradiction lanes)
The event is the entry point; the *hidden reason or smart observation* is the content. Rules:
1. The Short must still make sense 6–12 months later.
2. Wait 2–5 days before producing — let the news cycle settle.
3. Never take a *party-political* side (government / party / community / electoral).
4. Cover the hidden mechanism or the smart observation.
5. The event is the door. The reveal is the content.

### (c) Viral debate (Viral Social Commentary lane)
The *debate itself* is the content. Use when the internet is currently arguing about a specific viral object (a service, a price, a behaviour, a job listing, an app feature) and the audience verdict is the payoff. Rules:
1. The 6–12-month-relevance rule does *not* apply. The Short is produced for the live argument.
2. Wait 2–3 days before producing — let both sides crystallise.
3. Never take a *party-political* side. *Socio-economic debate* (wage math, labour arbitrage, pricing fairness, platform-labour conditions) is allowed and required.
4. Name both sides as steelman arguments before naming the discomfort.
5. Quote the viral source directly — job listing language, app copy, post text — where the specificity is the proof.
6. End on a stakes-naming viewer question + named-audience share; not on an aphorism.

### Shared limits across (b) and (c)
- Maximum 2 current-event Shorts per week (combined across both).
- Never two consecutive current-event Shorts.
- If a topic could be either (b) or (c), apply the Viral Social Commentary tiebreaker in `../00_SHARED_KB/FORMAT_LANES.md` § Format Selection Rules.

## Science Topics

Strong science Shorts:

- Universal phenomena the viewer has personally experienced (mosquitoes, sleep, food)
- Mechanisms that reveal an invisible process
- Counterintuitive biology / physics with practical implications
- Tech/science quirks with everyday relevance (phone cameras, soap dispensers)

Avoid:

- Pure curiosity facts with no payoff
- Topics requiring deep prerequisites to understand
- Speculative or fringe science presented as settled

## Historical / Biographical Stories

Strong history Shorts:

- Forgotten individuals whose story has a sharp angle
- Decisions whose consequences only became clear later
- Events where the conventional explanation is incomplete
- Stories with a clean "what really happened" reveal

Avoid:

- Generic biographies of well-known figures
- "Forgotten genius" stories with no real curiosity gap
- Politically risky figures without a clean editorial angle

## Rise-and-Fall Stories

Strong examples: Nokia, South Korea, Japan's lost decade, Argentina's decline.

Test:

- Is there a clean turning point, hidden reason, or natural transferable model?
- Can the Short land the answer inside the Anchor Shorts envelope without losing the viewer?
- Is the angle sharper than the surface story most viewers already know?

If yes, the topic works. If no clean model appears, leave it out instead of forcing one.

## Final Rule

A Short fails the creative gate only if it fails the **8-Point Shorts Validation**. It may still be downgraded or dropped by fact-risk, clone-risk, saturation, or the explicit auto-downgrade rules. Mental model is an optional bonus. Stop forcing live viral debates into the "hidden mechanism" frame — if the debate IS the content, route to Viral Social Commentary.
