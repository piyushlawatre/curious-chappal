- **Strongest source-backed narrative asset:** In 1979, a 16-year-old Jadav Payeng found hundreds of snakes dead on a treeless Brahmaputra sandbar — killed by heat with no shade. The termites and ants burrowed into the hard sand, making it porous and retaining water; the earthworms decomposed the bamboo leaf litter into rudimentary soil. Only once the soil existed could trees take hold. 40 years later: 1,360 acres, 120 bird species, rhinoceros, elephants, Bengal tigers.

- **Pictureability Test:** Editor can show: a bare sandbar on a wide river; a boy's hands releasing ants into hard sand; bamboo growing in a grid of earthen pots dripping water; over time, soil forming, shade appearing, then trees, then animals. The thesis (soil before forest) never has to be written on screen — the sequence shows it.
- **Narrative proposition:** Jadav Payeng did not plant a forest. He first engineered the ecological conditions — living soil — that made a forest possible, beginning with insects rather than seeds.

Just sand. → *Development:* A 16-year-old plants bamboo — not to grow a forest, but to create shade. Then he starts carrying ants from the mainland. Termites next. Earthworms. They burrowed into the sand, making it porous. Bamboo leaves fell and rotted into the first layer of actual soil. → *Turn:* Nobody told him to do this. The forest service said the land was too far gone. He continued anyway, alone, for four decades. → *Mechanism:* Insects are what made the soil. Soil is what made the trees possible. The trees made the shade. The shade brought the animals. He did not plant a forest — he assembled the parts a forest needs in the order a forest actually needs them. → *Payoff:* Today the Molai Forest has rhinoceros, elephants, and Bengal tigers. It is bigger than Central Park. One person built it. He started with ants.
