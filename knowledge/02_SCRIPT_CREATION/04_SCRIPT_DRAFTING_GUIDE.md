# Script Drafting Guide (Narrative Functions)

Canonical source for script inputs, required narrative functions, drafting rules, and the final script output template.

## Input Required From Upstream Brief

Before writing any Short, extract these from the Stage 1 brief:

- Topic
- Format lane
- Hook angle / hook candidates
- Payoff
- Source notes / fact-risk
- Indian relevance, if natural
- Mental model / framework from Stage 1 (optional; do not invent during drafting)
- Strongest source-backed narrative asset + its source
- Concrete story carrier + Pictureability Test
- Narrative proposition, intended story spine, and strongest safe keeper line

If the Stage 1 brief lacks a verdict, lane, corrected reframe when required, or a usable factual/source basis, Stage 2 should stop per `01_PROMPT.md`. If a brief from an older KB version lacks newer narrative fields such as the concrete story carrier, infer them from the verified brief without inventing facts and record the inference.

## How to Find the Angle

There are **two valid angle shapes** — pick the one the topic actually has, do not force the other.

**Shape A — Hidden Mechanism (Real Reason / Hidden India / Smart Money / Science Lite / Sharp Contradiction lanes).**
Name the viewer's surface belief, then the hidden reason / contradiction / missing mechanism that breaks it. The payoff is a single memorable line — the line the viewer repeats to a friend.

**Shape B — Live Debate (Viral Social Commentary lane).**
Name the viral object, then the two sides of the current argument (steelman both), then the discomfort that emerges when both sides are true. The payoff is the **viewer question + named-audience share** — the audience's verdict, not the host's aphorism. Do not invent a "hidden mechanism" behind the debate when the debate itself is the content.

For full debate-lane rules, use `../00_SHARED_KB/VIRAL_SOCIAL_COMMENTARY.md`. If a script in this lane lacks Side A, Side B, wage/price/behaviour math, direct viral evidence where available, and a viewer-verdict close, it is structurally unfinished no matter how polished the prose sounds.

The topic is ready in either shape when Stage 1 has identified a sharp hook stack and a payoff deliverable inside the Anchor Shorts envelope. If Stage 1 identified a natural transferable mental model, carry it forward. Never force a model during drafting.

## How to Avoid Heavy / Academic Writing

Strict rules during drafting:

- Calibrate every script against `../00_SHARED_KB/REFERENCE_SCRIPTS.md § Anchor Short References`.
- No greetings, "Did you know", channel intro, or "wait until the end".
- No banned hype words from `../00_SHARED_KB/LANGUAGE_AND_VOICE.md`.
- No forced mental model. Use the Stage 1 model; do not invent one during drafting.
- No documentary narrator / magazine-article register.
- No paragraph blocks where spoken vertical lines are needed.
- **Compression trap:** Brevity rules apply to filler, repetition, and setup padding. They do NOT apply to load-bearing causal steps, *and they do not apply to load-bearing debate evidence* (both sides' numbers, both sides' arguments, the wage / price / behaviour math). If removing a line would cause a viewer to miss a step in the causal chain — or miss either side of a debate — keep the line. Punchiness without logical completeness produces a caption, not a Short.
- **Vocabulary ceiling (mandatory):** The assumed viewer is a **Class 10–12 educated Indian metro reader**, not an engineer or MBA. Before returning any draft, run the Plain Word Test from `../00_SHARED_KB/LANGUAGE_AND_VOICE.md § Reader Benchmark`: scan every line and ask "would I use this word in a WhatsApp voice note to a college friend?" If no, replace it or explain it immediately. Prefer the same line; the next short line may define it when no other idea intervenes.
- **Instant Comprehension Gate (mandatory, phrase-level):** The Plain Word Test catches hard *words*; it does not catch hard *phrasing*. A line built from easy words can still be unclear on first listen if it carries source-register phrasing. Before returning any draft, run the Instant Comprehension Gate from `../00_SHARED_KB/LANGUAGE_AND_VOICE.md § The Instant Comprehension Gate` on every line: *"Will an average Indian viewer understand this immediately, on first listen, without pausing, Googling, or needing outside context?"* If no — rewrite into viewer-language per `§ Source-Language vs Viewer-Language`: move citation furniture (journal names, study IDs) to on-screen text, gloss any institution the viewer cannot place ("the Smithsonian" → "the Smithsonian, America's biggest museum group"), and replace report register ("records the outcome plainly") with spoken register. **Translate source wording, do not transplant it.** The frozen source notes protect facts, not phrasing — re-phrasing is required, not optional, as long as the fact stays exact.
- **Mental Math and Reference Stability (mandatory):** Plain words can still require too much mental work. Use at most one numerical relationship in the first five seconds. Do not place adjacent ratios with different denominators/reference groups unless the second line explicitly resets the comparison. Move the second number later or on-screen.
- **Hook-shape tie-breaker (formerly "story-before-receipts"):** The sharp declarative claim is the reference-majority opening and is always a valid default — it is never a defect to "upgrade." When Stage 1 names a verified scene, person, decision, conflict, object, or contrast, treat it as a **co-equal candidate**, not the default: sketch both the declarative open and the asset-led open, keep whichever creates stronger immediate curiosity at lower cognitive load, and note the choice in one line. Dropping the named asset from the script entirely (hook, setup, and mechanism) still requires a one-line justification. Numbers support the story; a receipt with no tension is not a hook.
- **Concrete-carrier gate:** Use the Stage 1 concrete story carrier to make the mechanism visible. A carrier may be a sourced scene or a clearly illustrative viewer-world scene/comparison that invents no specific event, quote, motive, company action, or measured result. A source finding or thesis is not a carrier merely because it is true. When the mechanism depends on comparison, selection, or relative price, show the options or reference point. Run the Pictureability Test: could the editor show the carrier without writing the thesis itself on screen? If no, rebuild it.

If any of these slip in, rewrite.

## Drafting Rule: Simple Mechanism, Simple English

The audience assumption is fixed (`../00_SHARED_KB/LANGUAGE_AND_VOICE.md § Below-Average English Comprehension Rule`): a curious, modern viewer who may have below-average English comfort. During drafting, explain the mechanism in simple cause-effect steps.

Use this structure:

1. What was the problem?
2. Who noticed it?
3. What did they do differently?
4. Why did it work?
5. What changed because of it?

Do not use abstract mechanism words if a simple sentence can explain the same idea.

Bad:

`Surat captured the low-value processing segment through labour-cost arbitrage and community credit.`

Better:

`A stone that lost money in Belgium made money in India. They polished in rupees. And sold in dollars. But it was not only cheap work. It was family networks.`

Before finalizing the draft, remove:

- trade jargon
- business jargon
- academic words
- long comma chains
- abstract mechanism phrasing
- clever lines that are not instantly understandable

Cushion every hard name on first mention (`In Belgium, there is a city called Antwerp.`) and never stack multiple unfamiliar names in one line.

**Final formatting:** deliver the script as anchor-readable beat blocks. One narrative beat per block; each block holds 1–4 short sentences that belong to the same beat; a new block starts when the story turns; blank line between blocks. Never one dense paragraph — and never one sentence per block throughout (that is the fragment-poem failure). The anchor must be able to read each block in one breath, with hook, reveal, mechanism, scale, and payoff visually separated. Calibration fixture: `../03_AUDIT_AND_FINALIZE/KB_REGRESSION_TESTS.md § Case 25`.

## High-Retention Indian Shorts Spoken Rhythm (canonical, v2.12)

Target: premium Indian YouTube Shorts with Anchor-level restraint and payoff. The script is a teleprompter, not an article. Anchor Short References are the voice/rhythm target. A Short should read like a smart Indian metro friend telling you the most interesting version of a true thing — at scroll speed, with rhythm, not paragraphs.

The system fails when a script is factually clean but reads as a long-form documentary-explainer or magazine-article paragraph chopped into short lines. Apply the rules below to all Anchor Shorts. The audit rubric in `../03_AUDIT_AND_FINALIZE/03_AUDIT_RUBRIC.md` (the VOICE check + Required Evidence (a)–(d)) enforces these rules with quoted-evidence checks — so a script that misses any of them will be routed back to rewrite even when the structure and facts are clean.

### Hook (single-line or stack — both fully valid)

An Anchor Short opens with either a sharp single-line declarative or a short 2–3 line stack. **Neither is the default; the topic decides.** A single strong declarative is often the more premium choice — most of the reference scripts use one. Use a stack only when killing the obvious answer across two lines genuinely builds more curiosity than one line would. A single-line hook is never a defect to "upgrade" to a stack.

Whichever shape you use, line two must **prove, sharpen, or open the causal promise**. Concrete support must arrive immediately after. Never use abstract restatement.

### Scene-First Hook (first-class shape)

A scene-first hook opens on a sourced person/place/action whose conflict creates the curiosity gap. Use it when the source gives the viewer something concrete to picture.

The test is not "does it contain a number?" The test is: "does the viewer immediately see a conflict and need the explanation?"

- Keep the scene source-backed. Do not invent motives or dialogue.
- Bring current numbers later, after the viewer cares.
- At least one of the three Stage 1 hook candidates should use this shape when a narrative asset exists.

### Viewer-World Comparison Hook (first-class shape)

A viewer-world comparison hook opens on a familiar, clearly illustrative scene or object contrast that makes the mechanism visible before the explicit explanation.

Use it when the topic is about an everyday choice, price, trust signal, incentive, or behaviour and no sourced historical scene is needed to carry the opening.

- The comparison must be ordinary and plausible, not presented as a documented event.
- It may take two or three short lines to create the picture before the claim lands.
- The contrast itself must create the causal question. Decorative B-roll does not count.
- Do not invent motives. Show the choice, then explain the sourced mechanism.

✅ "Two snack packets sit on the same shelf with the same job. / One says chocolate cookie. / The other says high protein cookie."
❌ "A shopper sees a protein cookie and happily pays twice as much." (invented reaction and unsupported number)

**Hook survivability (mandatory).** The hook overturns a belief, but it must not claim more than the body's facts support. Before locking it, ask: can this opening line survive the single most-informed viewer in the comments? If the hook over-claims — calling a prototype "the iPhone three years early", or a partial trend "streaming went back to weekly episodes" — soften the hook, not the facts. For this audience, an over-claimed hook turns the correction into the top comment and reframes the whole video.

✅ "Britain's most decorated female spy in the Second World War was an Indian princess. / Noor Inayat Khan was the great-great-granddaughter of Tipu Sultan." (single-line + proof)
✅ "Your ceiling fan does not cool the room. It cools you. / The moment you leave, the room gets slightly warmer." (short stack, no hype)
❌ "You won't believe what your ceiling fan is actually doing." (hype, no specific, banned opening)

### One Cognitive Anchor Per Line (setup discipline)

In the setup beat, do not stack unrelated specifics in one line. Give the viewer one cognitive anchor or action per line. Related details that form one instantly understandable action may stay together.

✅ "At CERN, scientists smashed lead ions together at almost the speed of light. / And for a tiny fraction of a second... / they created a temperature of over 5 trillion degrees."
❌ "CERN's ALICE experiment smashed lead ions at 99.9% the speed of light. Temperature produced: over 5 trillion degrees — more than 300,000 times hotter than the Sun's core."

Citation furniture used only to prove a claim ("WHO says...", "BIS says...", "Guinness certified...") normally does not belong in the setup beat. Move it into the reveal or fact-check notes. A story-central person, institution, or action may remain in the hook/setup when it is the story rather than citation furniture.

### First-Five-Seconds Number Limit

- Maximum one ratio, percentage relationship, or numerical comparison in the first five seconds.
- Never switch denominator/reference group between adjacent hook lines.
- A second useful number moves later or becomes on-screen support.

### Vertical Line Count

Write for the ear: one spoken thought per line, with single-line punches where they land. **There is no fixed line count** — the references are paragraph-shaped prose with periodic one-line punctuation. The real test is the 3-Line Article-Feel Test below: if your draft contains paragraphs of 2+ sentences glued together that join into magazine prose, break them. Do not pad to hit a count; do not fragment healthy sentences into stubs.

### Curiosity Ladder (retention by information, not by tics)

After the hook, the story must keep turning — a new fact or a real contradiction every few seconds, so the viewer always wants the next line. Retention comes from the information advancing, not from filler tease lines.

Do not manufacture curiosity with reaction-tease tics ("But honestly?", "Here's the twist.", "Wait for it.", "Sounds boring."). These are banned creator tics (see `../00_SHARED_KB/LANGUAGE_AND_VOICE.md § Banned Creator Tics`). The turn is the new fact itself: a flat pivot sentence ("That is not what happened.") followed immediately by the real reason carries the viewer without any tease.

### Spoken Texture (restrained, never performed)

The texture is a sharp Indian reader telling you the punchline of a long article — not a hype creator reacting. Use these spoken devices sparingly, and only where they land:

- **Single-sentence paragraphs as punctuation.** "That is not what happened." / "So India waits." Used rarely, they land because the lines around them are normal length.
- **Sentence fragments for emphasis.** "Before atoms. Before stars." Allowed; do not overuse.
- **One parallel triplet per script, maximum.** "Not one name. Not one location. Not one contact." A triplet is a rhythm device, not a signature — one per script is plenty, and never in the same structural beat as a reference triplet.

Banned (reaction-acting tics, not spoken rhythm): one/two-word reaction lines like "Bas.", "Right?", "Exactly.", "Sounds boring." No Hindi or Hinglish anywhere — complete Indian English only. If a line feels weak and the fix that comes to mind is a Hindi word or a reaction beat, the line is weak; fix the English.

### Spoken Connectives Stay

Do not cut "And", "But", "So", "Because" from line-starts. These are spoken-rhythm load-bearing. The "Sentence Style" rule about cutting unnecessary words applies to **written-language filler** (in other words, basically, it should be noted that), **not** to spoken-rhythm connectives. Do not add reaction-tease connectives ("Honestly?", "Right?") — those are banned tics, not load-bearing rhythm.

✅ "And what comes out is something called quark-gluon plasma."
✅ "But this is not normal matter."
❌ "What comes out is called quark-gluon plasma." (functionally identical, rhythmically dead)

### Fact-Safe Drama as Emotional Amplifier

Soft hedges — "scientists believe", "appears to", "estimates suggest", "reportedly" — are the correct way to phrase **causal or analytical inferences that the source itself does not state as fact**. They are not a substitute for a source.

Use a hedge only when you can name what is uncertain about the claim. If you cannot name the uncertainty, either find a source that supports the claim plainly, or cut the claim. "Scientists believe X" with no actual scientific source behind X is invention dressed as caution — that's a defect, not a feature.

**Soft hedges are NOT default for direct quotes.** Job-listing language, app copy, viral-post text, wage figures, and on-the-record prices should be quoted verbatim when the specificity itself is the viral payload. In Viral Social Commentary Shorts, replacing the original quote with a soft-hedged paraphrase is a defect, not a safety feature. *"9-hour days, standing and walking for long hours in crowded markets"* is the proof; *"worker pay is a question that matters"* is the laundered substitute. Use the first.

✅ "Scientists believe the early universe was filled with this same kind of matter just after the Big Bang." (source-backed uncertainty, factually safer)
❌ "Quark-gluon plasma is exactly what the universe was made of in its first microsecond after the Big Bang." (technically defensible, emotionally smaller, overclaims certainty)

### Payoff Verb Register

Prefer verbs that describe what actually happened, in spoken-language register. Avoid grandiose verbs that overstate the science ("rebuilt", "conquered", "destroyed", "shattered"). Avoid poetic verbs that introduce a claim the source does not make. If the metaphor adds emotional colour without adding a factual statement, it can stay; if it implies something the source does not say, cut it.

### The 3-Line Article-Feel Test (mandatory before returning)

Pick any 3 consecutive lines from your draft. Join them into a paragraph in your head. Does the paragraph read like a long-form documentary-explainer / magazine-article / Wikipedia-summary paragraph?

If yes — you have written an article. Break it, add connectives, add a reaction beat, add a fragment.

Run this test on the densest 3 lines of the reveal. That is where the drift always hides.

### Spoken-Rhythm Completion Test (drafting-owned items only)

Before submitting, confirm the five rhythm items that drafting owns. Everything else — repetition discipline, authority placement, hedge discipline, payoff verb register, self-clone vs the ledger — is Stage 3's audit; do not pre-run it here (per `01_PROMPT.md § Writer's Lock`).

- single-line hook or 2–3 line stack, with line two proving, sharpening, or opening the causal promise; no over-claim; at most one numerical relationship in the first five seconds (Writer's Lock 1)
- one cognitive anchor or action per setup line; no citation furniture in setup
- written for the ear: no glued 2+-sentence paragraph blocks AND no fragment-poem — run the 3-Line Article-Feel Test on the densest reveal lines
- retention by information: a new fact or real contradiction every few seconds; no reaction-tease tics, no Hinglish
- if Viral Social Commentary: Side A, Side B, the wage/price/behaviour math, direct viral evidence, and the three-beat close are present — with Beat 5 in topic-native wording, never the "inside their own X" reference construction

If any item is not satisfied, rewrite the affected beat.

## Credibility, Story, and Freshness Rules

These protect the script in the comments and keep the slate from cloning itself.

**Make authority checkable; place it for the viewer.** Never use "studies show", "researchers found", or "experts say" as proof. Speak a recognizable or story-central authority. When unfamiliar names add no first-listen value, speak the study shape ("a five-experiment 2020 study") and put the full citation on-screen and in fact-check notes.

**Preserve the narrative proposition, asset, and spine.** The asset need not literally be line one; a stronger contradiction may open and hand directly into it. A draft that replaces the strongest story spine with receipts must justify why the new treatment is more watchable.

**Attribute contested claims; do not state them as settled.** On any topic with a live academic or historical debate (famines, colonial history, disputed economics), present the interpretation as a named argument, not as fact. "Economist Amartya Sen argued, in a reading historians still debate, that…" buys credibility for five words and pre-empts the correction.

**Repetition discipline.** Say the thesis once with force, restate it once at the close, cut everything in between. See `../00_SHARED_KB/LANGUAGE_AND_VOICE.md § Repetition Discipline`. Surplus restatements both pad the back third and create the self-clone shape. Also vary the closer shape across the slate (§ Closer Variety).

**Self-clone check.** Strip the proper nouns from your draft and compare the skeleton against the last several Shorts. If the hook shape, the pivot phrasing, and the closer shape all match a recent script, rebuild from this topic's own contradiction. The live cloning risk is the channel cloning itself, not an external creator.

**Saturation check.** If the topic is one of the internet's most-covered explainers (the McDonald's real-estate take, Netflix binge-vs-weekly, "Nokia missed the iPhone"), lead with the genuinely lesser-known hook and cut the familiar setup — otherwise the ceiling is capped by "I've seen this".

**Verify hard numbers before lock.** Every specific figure presented as fact (rent totals, outlet counts, applicant numbers, dates) carries a source + date in the fact-check notes. Numbers that cannot be verified are softened or cut, not published.

## Required Narrative Functions

Every Short performs these functions. Their order is flexible when a topic-native story spine creates stronger clarity and retention.

### Beat 1: Hook Stack (0–5 seconds)
The hook may be one sharp line or a 2-3 line stack. It must create a curiosity gap before context begins.

Best default shape:
1. Attack or reverse the common assumption.
2. Reject the obvious answer.
3. Reveal the specific place, person, number, or mechanism.

The hook should feel spoken, not headlined. A sharp question is allowed only when the question itself creates the curiosity gap.

### Beat 2: Setup (3–10 seconds)
Quickly establish the situation, the source, or the stakes. Just enough that the viewer trusts what's coming. Concrete numbers / names / places preferred.

**Surface Logic Depth rule (Real Reason and Sharp Contradiction lanes):** Before the contradiction, build the viewer's intuitive belief fully. Name every reasonable argument for their side. The more complete the setup, the more powerful the reversal. "Just enough context" does not mean minimal — it means the precise amount that makes the viewer feel they are correct before you contradict them.

✅ Four lines building the transparent-tank case (see level, see dirt, catch motor overflow, seems logical) → then contradiction.
❌ Two lines ("you can see the level, you can see the dirt") → immediate contradiction. Setup is too thin to create reversal force.

### Function 3: The Turn in Understanding
The viewer must feel the story change direction. A flat pivot sentence may do this, but it is not mandatory when a decision, scene, or causal contrast already performs the turn naturally.

Prefer a **topic-native pivot** when available: a line that changes the organising picture and gives the next beat somewhere concrete to go. "But that is only the inside story" is stronger for a packet-pricing script than a generic "But that is not the whole story." Do not force a clever metaphor; use the topic's own object, action, or contrast.

### Beat 4: The Reveal / Mechanism (10–45 seconds)
The body of the Short. Walk the viewer through what actually happened, the hidden reason, or the surprising mechanism. Keep it fast, clear, and easy to follow at scroll speed.

**Multi-Mechanism rule:** If the topic has two or more distinct causal mechanisms, map their causal roles, such as ENABLE → TRIGGER → CONVERT → CLOSE. Each role must be clear, but explicit "First/Second" labels are optional and should appear only when they improve comprehension.

**Observable-Behaviour rule:** When the mechanism concerns a human choice, incentive, trust signal, price, or habit, show at least one short action chain the viewer can observe. Translate the abstract mechanism into what a person sees, does, skips, or chooses.

✅ "They scan. They eliminate. They pick the option that feels easiest to defend."
❌ "The label reduces cognitive effort and increases willingness to pay." (correct idea, unstaged mechanism)

### Beat 5: The Indian-Relevance Bridge (only when natural)
One concrete lived condition, familiar contrast, or practical behaviour — never a label, never a tacked-on "and in India…". Four of the sixteen references carry no Indian framing at all; if the bridge does not sharpen the Short, omit the beat entirely. See `../00_SHARED_KB/LANGUAGE_AND_VOICE.md § The Indian-relevance bridge (Beat 5)`.

### Beat 6: The Payoff + Transferable Insight (45–60 seconds)
The clear takeaway. There are **two valid payoff shapes** — pick the one the lane requires.

**Shape A — Aphoristic payoff (Hidden-Mechanism lanes: Real Reason / Hidden India / Smart Money / Science Lite / Sharp Contradiction).**
A single memorable line the viewer remembers and repeats:

- A sharp summary of the reveal
- A clean observation
- A surprising data point
- A relatable closer
- A transferable principle / mental model from Stage 1 — never forced; include it only when the mechanism earned it (see `../00_SHARED_KB/MASTER_RULE.md` mental-model rule), kept light unless the topic earns the emphasis

**Shape B — Audience-verdict payoff (Viral Social Commentary lane).**
The close is the lane's mandatory three-beat block: a compressed insight naming the current structural shift, then the **viewer question** that crystallises the debate, then the named-audience share. The compressed insight is not a Shape-A aphorism or universal lesson. The question must:

- Name the *specific stakeholder* the viewer would actually decide for ("for your parents", "for your wedding", "for ₹18,000/month")
- Be answerable as "yes / no / it depends" — not open-ended
- Be followed by a named-audience share prompt as part of the same close

Do *not* paste a Shape-A aphorism or universal lesson onto a Viral Social Commentary Short and then bolt the viewer question on after. Keep only the dated structural-shift insight required by the lane.

**Accuracy-over-catchiness rule (Shape A):** A payoff that is memorable but inaccurate is a defect, not a feature. If the real answer is more nuanced than a single line allows, add one additional line stating the nuance before the punchline. The punchline compresses the truth — it does not distort it.

**Op-Ed Aphorism test (Shape B):** read the closing 2 lines aloud. Do they sound like a New York Times sub-head or a magazine kicker ("India did not invent the helper; it priced the helper")? If yes, you've drifted into Shape A. Rewrite to a viewer question.

✅ "Black is not magic. White tanks can also work — if they block sunlight. / The real rule is not colour. The real rule is: sunlight should not enter your stored water. / The tank is not wearing fashion. The tank is wearing sunscreen."
❌ Implying "black = always correct" without stating the actual principle — leaving the viewer with a new oversimplification.

### Beat 7: The Hook-Back / Identity / Closing Line (final 10–15 seconds)
A line that lands the channel's voice. Casual. Confident. Complete Indian English. This is where our identity shows up — language, framing, attitude.

### Optional CTA (not a beat — final 5–8 seconds)
Tied to content. Specific stakes, specific action. May be omitted on Shorts where the payoff is the close.

## Hook Stack Rules

- Lands in 1–5 seconds
- May be one line or a 2-3 line stack
- Attacks/reverses an assumption, or stages a concrete comparison whose contrast creates the causal question before context
- Declarative-first (or a sharp curiosity-question)
- Specific (numbers, names, places preferred)
- Overturns or sharpens an assumption
- No greeting, no channel name, no intro, no hype words

## Mid-Script Retention Rules

- New information every 3–6 seconds
- At least one specific number, name, or source
- Pacing varies — slow on important beats, fast through familiar context
- Allow a half-second land beat after a major reveal
- Use specific data, not vague claims

## Indian / South Asian Relevance Rules

Apply Indian context only when it naturally belongs:

✅ The topic is Indian
✅ Indian audience has personal stakes
✅ Indian comparison sharpens the reveal
✅ Indian lived experience adds weight (or humour, or relatability)

❌ Tacking on "and in India..." paragraphs to global stories
❌ Adding Indian examples that feel stretched
❌ Forcing *party-political* comparisons that could be read as critical of a government or party (socio-economic debate — labour arbitrage, pricing, wage math — is in-scope when the topic is in the Viral Social Commentary lane)
❌ Mentioning India just to seem locally relevant

**Indian context must be concrete, not labelled:** Naming "Indian terrace" is insufficient. Concrete Indian context means: a named contrast (not an AC showroom), a specific lived condition (direct sun, years of exposure, no shade), a relatable practical behaviour (checking motor overflow). Generic Indian labels without concrete specifics earn zero Indian-relevance points.

✅ "Your tank is not sitting in an AC showroom. It is sitting under direct sunlight, every day, for years."
❌ "Terrace pe, this becomes a problem." (labels but does not picture)

## Ending Rules

The ending must do one of:

1. Land the payoff as a sharp, memorable line
2. Drop a clean observation that closes the loop
3. Deliver an identity line in our voice — dry, casually confident, complete Indian English
4. *(Optional)* Name a transferable principle the topic produces

The ending must NEVER:

- Ask a generic question (vague "what do you think?" with no specific stakes named — banned in every lane)
- Summarise what was just said
- Tell viewers to subscribe
- Tease the next video
- Drift into motivational / LinkedIn-style wisdom
- Force a mental model where none naturally exists
- Add any explanatory or summarising line after the final punchline (Punchline Protection applies in all lanes, but in Viral Social Commentary the *viewer question* is the punchline — see below)

**Viral Social Commentary lane — ending carve-out:** the natural ending of a debate-lane Short is a *specific stakes-naming viewer question* + a *named-audience share prompt*. The question IS the punchline; the share line is part of it, not a follow-on. Example shape: *"Would you use this for your parents? Share this with somebody who needs to see this."* A generic "what do you think?" remains banned; a question that names the specific stakeholder ("for your parents", "for ₹18,000/month", "before your wedding") is encouraged.

**Punchline Protection rule:** The final punchline or identity line is the last spoken line of the script. Nothing follows it. If the punchline requires setup to land, that setup comes before the punchline — never after. An explanatory sentence after the punchline is not a CTA; it is a dilution. Cut it.

**Ending Subtraction Test:** remove each closing line in turn. If the ending remains clear and lands harder, cut that line. The strongest final line gets the final emphasis.

❌ "The tank is wearing sunscreen. Next time you see one, don't call it dull. It is doing the boring work properly." — three lines when one sufficed.
✅ "The tank is not in a beauty contest. It is wearing sunscreen." — clean stop.

## CTA Rules

Most Shorts should end on the payoff with **no spoken CTA** — nothing follows the final line (Punchline Protection). The only spoken CTA permitted is the Viral Social Commentary three-beat close (compressed insight + stakes-naming viewer question + named-audience share). A standard subscribe/follow nudge is allowed only in the **video description, pinned comment, or an end-card — never in the voiceover.** See `../00_SHARED_KB/LANGUAGE_AND_VOICE.md § CTAs § Spoken Script vs Description / End-Card`.

## Fact-Check Rules

Use `../00_SHARED_KB/SOURCE_AND_FACT_RULES.md`. This bar does not relax because Shorts are lighter: every load-bearing claim needs a source tier label. Supporting context (commonly-known facts, well-established background) does not.

## Length Rules

- **Anchor Short:** 60–120 seconds spoken, 200–280 words (hard ceiling 280).
- **Minimum:** 90 seconds.
- **Hard ceiling:** never run past 120 seconds for any Short.

Use **60–105 seconds** when the topic is a single sharp fact, contradiction, or observation.
Use **105–120 seconds** when the topic needs more breathing room, a stronger build, or a more meaningful payoff.

Pick the shortest length that still lands the payoff. If a topic needs more than 120 seconds, plan long-form instead.

## Sentence Style

- Short, punchy sentences
- One idea per sentence
- Avoid compound sentences with multiple clauses
- Sentence fragments allowed for emphasis
- Spoken English — not written English
- Ear-first prose, not a fragment poem: the references are paragraph-shaped prose with periodic single-line punches. Break glued 2+-sentence blocks, but never shatter healthy sentences into stub lines — a script chopped into dozens of fragments fails VOICE exactly as article prose does. The 3-Line Article-Feel Test is the check in both directions.
- Don't say "in other words" or restate
- Cut written-language filler (in other words, basically, it should be noted that). Keep spoken-language connectives ('And', 'But', 'So', 'Because') at line-starts even if technically removable — they are spoken-rhythm load-bearing. ('Right?', 'Honestly?', 'But honestly?' are reaction-tease tics, NOT connectives — banned per `../00_SHARED_KB/LANGUAGE_AND_VOICE.md § Banned Creator Tics`.)

Good: "Most of them failed. Real failures, real bankruptcies."
Good: "Nokia did not lose to the iPhone. Nokia lost to Nokia."
Bad: "Many of the companies were unable to compete and consequently went into bankruptcy."

## What to Do If the Topic Is Weak

Do not write a Short that lacks a clean hook + payoff + reveal. Return to Stage 1 for a reframe or drop. Do not invent a transferable framework during drafting just to rescue a topic.
