## 8. Final Spoken Script

[direct] October in India. The monsoon is over.

Mumbai's streets are drying out. The umbrellas are packed away. The season has ended.

Cut to Chennai, same week. The city is watching the sky. The main rain has not come yet.

[pause]

Every Indian learns one monsoon. June to September, sweeping in from the Arabian Sea. It fills school lessons and weather forecasts. Mumbai, Delhi, Kolkata — every city the national media watches — runs on this system. Farms, reservoirs, and the agricultural calendar across most of India depend on it.

[beat]

Tamil Nadu's coast is the exception.

The southwest monsoon largely bypasses Tamil Nadu's eastern shore. The winds blow parallel to the coastline, carrying most of their rain to the Western Ghats and central India instead.

[beat]

But in October, [stress] something changes.

The land cools faster than the ocean. That difference flips the wind direction — from land toward sea, to sea toward land. Those reversed winds cross the Bay of Bengal, pick up moisture, and hit Tamil Nadu's coast from October to December.

This is the northeast monsoon. For Tamil Nadu's coastal cities — Chennai included — this is the main event. India's Meteorological Department data shows around 60 percent of their annual rainfall comes from this second system, not the first.

[pause]

[slow] The monsoon India knows ends in October.

[drop voice] For Chennai, that is when it begins.
