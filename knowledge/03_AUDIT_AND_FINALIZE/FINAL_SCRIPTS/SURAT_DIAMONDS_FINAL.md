## Final Spoken Script (278 words, ~105-112s)

[direct] In the 1960s, the diamond cutters of Antwerp kept sweeping certain stones aside.

Traders from Gujarat asked for [stress] exactly those stones.

Today, one Indian city cuts around nine of every ten of the world's diamonds. The city is Surat.

[beat]

For a century, Antwerp in Belgium ran the diamond trade. Picture the sorting table: one pile of big stones, worth days of an expensive cutter's wages. And a heap of tiny rough — uncut stones too small to be worth a European cutter's day.

[slow] That heap is where this story begins.

Gujarati trading families from the town of Palanpur arrived in Antwerp at the bottom of the business. The established houses had little interest in the small stones. So the newcomers bought them cheap and shipped them home to Surat.

[beat]

And there, the math flipped. A stone not worth touching on a Belgian wage turned a profit on a Surat wage. The traders polished in rupees and sold in dollars.

And the timing was perfect. Soviet mines in Siberia had begun flooding the market with huge volumes of those same tiny stones. The world now had mountains of rough that only one place could cut at a profit.

Workers came from Saurashtra's farming villages — families escaping drought — and sat at the polishing wheels. Cutters became factory owners within a generation.

[pause]

Fifty years later, Antwerp still trades diamonds. But the cutting lives in Surat — which has built itself a diamond office complex bigger than the Pentagon.

[beat]

Surat built a diamond empire out of the stones Europe refused to cut. The bottom of a market is an unguarded door.

[drop voice] The next diamond you see at a wedding was probably cut in Surat.
