## 8. Final Spoken Script

[direct] In 1979, a teenager found hundreds of snakes dead on a sandbar in the Brahmaputra.

[beat]

No shade. No soil. Just sand and heat.

[beat]

[slow] The first thing he brought to that sandbar was not a tree. It was ants.

[pause]

His name was Jadav Payeng. The sandbar was on Majuli — a river island in Assam. Floods had stripped the land bare. Nobody believed anything could grow there.

Payeng started with bamboo. About twenty seedlings, drilled into the sand with a stick. He carried water to them using earthen pots with small holes — each pot dripping slowly over several days so the roots could drink.

But bamboo alone would not hold. The sand had no organic matter in it. Trees need soil. Sand is not soil.

So he carried ants from the mainland. Termites next. Earthworms after that.

[beat]

The termites burrowed into the hard surface and made it porous. The ants worked the ground. The earthworms broke down the bamboo leaf litter into the first thin layer of real soil. The bamboo roots held the sand from washing away.

[slow] He had not planted a forest. He had assembled the parts a forest needs, in the order a forest actually needs them.

He did this alone, every day, for forty years.

[beat]

Today, that sandbar is 1,360 acres of living forest. It has over a hundred species of birds. Rhinoceros. Elephants. Bengal tigers. It is called the Molai Forest, named after Payeng. The Government of India gave him the Padma Shri in 2015.

[drop voice] He did not plant a forest. He planted what a forest needs first.
